02 · Types of Recommendation Systems

The Three Main Approaches

The field splits pretty cleanly into a few categories. Each one answers "what should I recommend?" in a different way — and each has a different idea of what it means to "know" a user.

👥

Collaborative Filtering

I recommend based on what similar users liked — no need to understand the content itself.

🔬

Content-Based Filtering

I analyze item features and recommend things similar to what you've already liked.

🔀

Hybrid Systems

I combine both approaches to get the benefits of each and reduce their individual weaknesses.

📖

Knowledge-Based

I use domain rules or explicit constraints — useful for rare or complex purchases like cars or homes.

Content-Based Filtering

The idea here is to look at features of items a user has liked and find other items that share those features. If I've watched a lot of Christopher Nolan films, a content-based system would identify common traits — complex plots, sci-fi themes, specific visual style — and suggest films with similar fingerprints.

The Cold Start Problem

One issue I kept running into while reading about this is the cold start problem. When a brand-new user signs up, or when a new item enters the catalog, there's no historical data to work with. Both content-based and collaborative approaches struggle here — and it's still an active area of research. I'll come back to it in the research paper section.

Types of Collaborative Filtering Techniques

The Technique I Find Most Interesting

Out of everything I've covered, collaborative filtering is the approach I find most elegant. The core idea: I don't need to understand what items are — I just need to understand who liked what. If two users have rated the same movies similarly, there's a good chance they'd agree on movies neither has seen yet.

There are two main families — memory-based and model-based — each with sub-approaches:

The User-Item Rating Matrix

Everything starts with a ratings matrix — rows are users, columns are items, cells contain ratings (or blanks for unrated items). The goal is to predict the missing values. This matrix is almost always extremely sparse; most users have rated only a small fraction of the whole catalog.

04 · Matrix Factorization

The Math Behind Latent Taste Factors

Matrix factorization was probably the most interesting concept I read about in this whole topic. The idea: there are hidden dimensions of taste — things like "preference for cerebral films" or "love of slow-burn dramas" — that explain the patterns in the ratings matrix. The algorithm discovers these automatically.

I decompose the large sparse ratings matrix R into two smaller matrices — a User matrix U and an Item matrix V — so their product approximates R:

R  ≈  U  ×  Vᵀ
RUsers × Items
(sparse matrix)
UUsers × Latent Factors
(user taste profile)
VᵀLatent Factors × Items
(item taste profile)

Alternating Least Squares (ALS)

In practice I use ALS to find U and V. It fixes one matrix and optimizes the other, then alternates back and forth until the prediction error stabilizes. It scales well — which is why platforms like Netflix and Spotify use it in production.

1
Initialize U and V randomly

Start with random values — they converge to meaningful latent factors over iterations.

2
Fix V, solve for best U

Holding the item matrix constant, find the user matrix that minimizes prediction error.

3
Fix U, solve for best V

Now hold the user matrix and optimize the item matrix instead.

4
Repeat until convergence

Alternate until total RMSE stops decreasing meaningfully.

5
Predict via R̂ = U × Vᵀ

The full product gives predicted ratings for every user-item pair, including unobserved ones.

Hybrid Recommendations

In practice the best systems combine approaches. Content-based helps when there's no interaction history (new users, new items); collaborative filtering picks up nuanced patterns that emerge over time. Netflix, Spotify, and YouTube all blend them together.

05 · Research Paper

The Amazon Paper That Started It All

📄 Academic Paper — Google Scholar

Item-to-Item Collaborative Filtering

Linden, G., Smith, B., & York, J. (2003). "Amazon.com Recommendations: Item-to-Item Collaborative Filtering." IEEE Internet Computing, 7(1), 76–80.

This is one of the most-cited applied ML papers ever written — not because of groundbreaking mathematics, but because it solved a real engineering problem at a scale that hadn't been tackled before.

What Problem Was Amazon Actually Solving?

By 2003, Amazon had millions of customers and millions of products. The classic approach — find users similar to you, recommend what they bought — simply doesn't scale. Computing similarity across tens of millions of users in real time is computationally impossible.

Instead of asking "which users are like this user?", Amazon flipped it: "which items are consistently bought together?" — a question that can be computed offline and answered instantly at runtime.

The Simple Idea Behind It

Imagine running a bookstore and keeping a notebook of which books are frequently bought together. "People who buy The Great Gatsby also buy Catcher in the Rye." That notebook — the item-item similarity table — is built once overnight and consulted instantly whenever any customer arrives. That's essentially what item-item CF does.

# Offline — precomputed nightly
for each item i in catalog:
    for each item j in catalog:
        similarity[i][j] = cosine(ratings_i, ratings_j)

# Online — milliseconds at runtime
def recommend(user):
    candidates = {}
    for item in user.purchase_history:
        candidates.update(similarity[item].top_k(10))
    return rank(candidates, exclude=user.purchase_history)

It also partially addresses the cold start problem: since recommendations are based on items a new user clicked on, not a full rating history, even first-time visitors get reasonable suggestions immediately.

06 · Real-World Case Study

Netflix — A Well-Documented Real-World System

NETFLIX

I picked Netflix because it's the most well-documented example in this space. Netflix has published detailed engineering blogs and research papers about its recommendation pipeline — which made it a practical one to look into rather than guess at.

The Netflix Prize

In 2006, Netflix released anonymized user rating data publicly and offered $1 million to anyone who could improve recommendation accuracy by 10%. The winning team won three years later by blending dozens of models together — SVD variants, neighborhood models, restricted Boltzmann machines. This ensemble approach is now standard industry practice.

How Netflix Recommends Things Today

Based on Netflix's published engineering research, their recommendation pipeline runs through four main stages:

1
Candidate Generation

Retrieve around 1,000 potentially relevant titles from a catalog of ~15,000 using fast embedding-based search.

2
Ranking

A neural ranking model scores each candidate using hundreds of signals: viewing history, time of day, device, trending content, recent taste shifts.

3
Page Assembly

Even the rows on the homepage are personalized — which rows appear and in what order is itself a recommendation problem.

4
Artwork Personalization

Netflix tests different thumbnails per user for the same show. A romance fan sees different artwork than an action fan for the exact same title.

Netflix Recommendation Signals — Estimated Relative Weights

What I found surprising is that Netflix weights implicit signals (watch duration, pause behavior, completion rate) far more than explicit ratings like thumbs up or down. What people do reveals more than what they say they like.

Netflix has publicly stated that its recommendation engine saves around $1 billion per year in reduced subscriber churn — which makes the engineering investment very easy to justify.

07 · Evaluation Methods

How I Know If the Recommender Is Working

Evaluating recommendation systems is genuinely harder than it looks. The metric I choose shapes the system I build — and many commonly used metrics end up measuring the wrong thing.

RMSE
Root Mean Squared Error
How far predicted ratings are from actual ones. Lower is better.
MAE
Mean Absolute Error
Average absolute gap between predicted and actual ratings.
P@K
Precision at K
Of the top K recommendations, what fraction are actually relevant?
R@K
Recall at K
Of all relevant items, what fraction appear in my top K?
NDCG
Discounted Cumulative Gain
Rewards putting the most relevant items at the top of the list.
MRR
Mean Reciprocal Rank
How high does the first truly relevant item appear?

Beyond Accuracy

A system that only recommends the most popular items will score well on accuracy metrics but isn't really doing anything useful — it's just showing the top charts. Three other dimensions matter:

DimensionWhat It MeasuresWhy It Matters
🎯 NoveltyHow unexpected are the recommendations?Recommending only blockbusters is lazy — good recommendations should surface things you wouldn't have found yourself.
🌈 DiversityHow different are items within the recommendation set?Ten near-identical suggestions is a bad experience even if each item scores well individually.
✨ SerendipitySurprising recommendations that turn out to be highly relevantSomething you didn't know you wanted, that you end up loving. This builds long-term user trust.
Accuracy vs. Diversity Tradeoff

Offline vs. Online Evaluation

The most important lesson here: offline metrics and real-world results can diverge significantly. The gold standard is always an online A/B test — split users into control and treatment groups, deploy the new model to treatment, and directly measure engagement, retention, or whatever you actually care about. No offline metric fully replaces that.

08 · Wrapping Up

What I Took Away

Going through this topic gave me a much better understanding of the systems running in the background of the apps I use every day. What looks like a simple "you might also like..." list is actually the output of a pipeline involving data collection, matrix decomposition, neural ranking, and continuous testing.

🧬 LLMs are reshaping this field

Large language models are enabling richer content understanding — moving beyond item IDs and ratings toward genuine semantic meaning.

🛡️ Privacy matters more now

Federated learning — keeping user data on-device — is becoming an important consideration as data regulations tighten.

⚖️ Filter bubbles are a real concern

Recommendation systems can reinforce popularity bias and narrow what people are exposed to. That's worth thinking about.

🎯 Picking the right metric is hard

Optimizing for accuracy alone leads to obvious, boring recommendations. Diversity and serendipity matter too.

I started this wanting to understand how Netflix knew what I should watch next. I ended up learning about a field that sits at the intersection of math, psychology, and engineering — and that's genuinely useful to understand.
